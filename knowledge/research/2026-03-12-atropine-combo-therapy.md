# Latest Research on Atropine Efficacy and Combination Therapy for Myopia Control (2026-03-12)

## Atropine Efficacy:
- **Optimal Concentration**: Low-dose atropine (0.01%-0.05%) is most effective. 0.05% shows approximately double the efficacy of 0.01% over two years, with sustained benefits over five years.
- **Progression Reduction**: A 2022 network meta-analysis ranked 0.05% atropine highest for reducing progression risk (RR 0.39; 95% CI 0.27–0.57), outperforming higher doses (like 1%) due to better tolerability.
- **Side Effects**: Minimal side effects with low-dose, such as slight pupil dilation (~1 mm) and reduced accommodation.
- **Key Trials**:
    - **ATOM1 (2006)**: 1% atropine reduced progression by 77%, but high side effects.
    - **ATOM2 (2012)**: 0.01% maintained efficacy with minimal side effects over five years.
    - **LAMP phase 2/3**: 0.05% was optimal, with five-year progressions of -1.34 D (vs. -2.34 D for 0.01%).
    - **CHAMP trial**: 0.01% slowed axial elongation by 0.13 mm vs. placebo.
- **Mechanism**: Atropine targets the retina/sclera, not accommodation.
- **Regulatory Notes**: Japan approved 0.025% atropine in 2024. US FDA rejected low-dose based on STAR trial data.

## Combination Therapy:
- **Enhanced Efficacy**: Combination therapy significantly enhances efficacy, especially for non-compliant patients or those with specific risks (e.g., esotropia).
- **Real-world Cases**: Adding 0.025% atropine to inconsistent orthokeratology or multifocal contacts stabilized progression and axial length over 24-30 months.
- **Esotropic Myopes**: Low-dose atropine can be paired with progressive addition lenses (PALs) or multifocal soft contacts to counteract cycloplegia-induced esotropia and reduce accommodative lag.
- **Emerging Therapies**: Emerging RLRL therapy shows short-term axial reductions comparable to 1% atropine, but long-term safety and rebound effects are uncertain. No direct combination data with atropine yet.

## Limitations:
- US trials showed waning benefits of 0.01% atropine after 18-24 months.
- Long-term data for RLRL therapy is needed.
- Adherence to treatment remains a challenge, highlighting the value of combination therapies.
